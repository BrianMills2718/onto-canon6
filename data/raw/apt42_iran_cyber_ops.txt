SOURCE 1: MANDIANT / GOOGLE TAG — APT42 ACTOR PROFILE
(Synthesized from "APT42: Crooked Charms, Cons, and Compromises" and
"Uncharmed: Untangling Iran's APT42 Operations", Google Cloud Blog)
================================================================================

APT42 is an Iranian state-sponsored cyber espionage group assessed with moderate-to-high
confidence to operate on behalf of the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps Intelligence
Organization (IRGC-IO). The group has operated continuously since at least 2015,
functioning as the Iranian regime's primary cyber unit focused on human-targeted
intelligence collection rather than infrastructure destruction. APT42 is tracked under
multiple names across the threat intelligence industry: Mandiant and Google Threat Analysis
Group designate it APT42; Microsoft tracks overlapping activity as Mint Sandstorm (formerly
Phosphorus); Proofpoint designates it TA453; other vendors use Charming Kitten, Yellow
Garuda, COBALT ILLUSION, or ITG18.

APT42 operations fall into three broad categories: credential harvesting, surveillance
operations, and malware deployment. Unlike related Iranian groups that conduct destructive
or financially motivated attacks, APT42 maintains a consistent focus on intelligence
collection with minimal-footprint operations targeting individuals and organizations of
strategic interest to the Iranian government.

TARGETING PROFILE

APT42 distinctly "targets people, not systems." The group specializes in surveilling
individuals perceived as threats or of intelligence value to Iranian interests. Primary
target categories include:

- Journalists covering Iran and Middle Eastern affairs
- Academic researchers and think tank analysts focused on foreign policy
- NGO personnel and human rights activists
- Iranian dissidents and diaspora members living abroad
- Government officials from adversarial nations, particularly the United States, Israel,
  and United Kingdom
- Political campaign operatives and senior government officials
- Legal services professionals handling Iran-related matters

Geographically, between February and late July 2024, APT42 intensified targeting of Israel
and the United States. As of 2024, the U.S. and Israel together accounted for roughly 60%
of the group's known geographic targeting activity. Western and Middle Eastern NGOs, media
organizations, academia, and legal services firms constitute the core institutional target
set.

SOCIAL ENGINEERING METHODOLOGY

APT42's operators are widely assessed to be among the most skilled social engineers in the
state-sponsored threat landscape. Their approach emphasizes patience over technical
exploits. The group is "willing to invest weeks or months cultivating relationships with
targets before attempting to steal credentials." Operators engage targets through "benign
conversations that may continue for days or weeks" before introducing credential harvesting
links disguised as something the target would expect to receive.

The group poses as journalists and event organizers to build trust with their victims
through ongoing correspondence, then delivers invitations to conferences or legitimate
documents, which enables APT42 to harvest credentials and use them to gain initial access
to cloud environments.

Specific impersonation targets documented by Mandiant and Recorded Future News include:

- The Washington Post (using the typosquatted domain "washinqtonpost[.]press")
- The Economist
- The Jerusalem Post
- Khaleej Times (UAE)
- Azadliq (Azerbaijan)
- The U.S. Aspen Institute
- The Washington Institute for Near East Policy
- The Royal United Services Institute (RUSI, UK)
- The Institute for the Study of War (ISW)

In one documented 2023 case, APT42 impersonated a RUSI senior fellow while contacting a
nuclear security expert at a U.S.-based foreign affairs organization. The attackers claimed
to be working on a project called "Iran in the Global Security Context," offered payment
for feedback, and had previously sent emails posing as other well-known nuclear security
experts named in the purported project, building cross-referencing credibility. When
initial malware failed on the victim's Apple computer, the attackers sent a follow-up
email with Mac-compatible malware, demonstrating operational adaptability.

CREDENTIAL HARVESTING INFRASTRUCTURE

APT42 operates sophisticated credential-harvesting kits designated GCollection (targeting
Google accounts), LCollection (targeting Hotmail/Microsoft accounts), and YCollection
(targeting Yahoo accounts). The group also deploys the DWP browser-in-the-browser kit.
Victims are directed to fake login pages that simultaneously capture passwords and
multi-factor authentication (MFA) tokens, defeating standard two-factor authentication.

The group abuses legitimate cloud services including Google Sites, Google Drive, Dropbox,
OneDrive, and URL shorteners to host phishing pages and redirect chains. This use of
trusted infrastructure complicates detection and blocking. Malicious domains are registered
using typosquatting, and the group also employs VPN services and anonymized infrastructure
to avoid attribution.

Specific observed infrastructure includes domains such as "mailer-daemon[.]live,"
"email-protection[.]online," and "gdrive-files[.]com." In one documented case, the domain
"DE-MA.ONLINE" was registered using personal information of an Israeli citizen, potentially
as a false flag to obscure Iranian origin.

MALWARE AND CUSTOM TOOLS

APT42 deploys two primary custom backdoors in addition to publicly available tools:

NICECURL (also called BASICSTAR): A backdoor written in VBScript that can download
additional modules to be executed on compromised hosts, including a datamining module.
It provides an arbitrary command execution interface and establishes persistent access.

TAMECAT: A PowerShell-based backdoor used for reconnaissance and payload delivery.
It executes arbitrary PowerShell or C# content and is used after initial access to
maintain persistence and stage further operations.

HYPERSCRAPE: A data extraction tool identified by Google TAG in 2022, designed to
systematically harvest content from Google, Yahoo, and Microsoft email accounts belonging
to compromised targets.

In 2024, APT42 introduced a more sophisticated malware toolkit called BlackSmith,
delivering a new PowerShell trojan called AnvilEcho. AnvilEcho comprises approximately
2,200 lines of code consolidating prior capabilities. It performs extensive system
reconnaissance, captures screenshots and audio recordings, harvests browser credentials
and cookies, executes remote commands, and exfiltrates data via FTP or Dropbox channels.
The BlackSmith toolkit's infection chain includes multiple components: soshi.dll (an
installer searching for supporting files), mary.dll (an in-memory payload loader and
decryption module), toni.dll (an antivirus evasion and persistence mechanism), and
videogui.exe (the final payload loader).

Post-compromise, APT42 collects data from Microsoft 365 cloud environments, harvests
browser credentials and cookies, captures screenshots, and discovers system and network
information. The group exfiltrates files to OneDrive accounts impersonating victim
organizations. Persistence mechanisms include OAuth permissions granted to attacker-
controlled applications, email forwarding rules, API key creation, and secondary account
compromise via connected services.

SOURCE 2: GOOGLE TAG / MANDIANT — 2024 U.S. ELECTION INTERFERENCE CAMPAIGN
(From Mandiant "Uncharmed" report and Google TAG, 2024; reported by The Hacker News,
HedgehogSecurity, and Anvilogic)
================================================================================

APT42's activities in 2024 extended to sustained, though ultimately unsuccessful, attempts
to compromise the personal accounts of individuals associated with major U.S. political
figures. Google TAG documented ongoing attempts to compromise accounts connected to
President Biden, Vice President Harris, and former President Trump.

Activity intensified beginning in April 2024 and peaked in May 2024. Between May and late
July 2024, APT42 targeted approximately twelve individuals across both the Trump and
Biden/Harris campaigns. The group achieved documented success against Trump campaign
personnel: attackers compromised Trump adviser Roger Stone's email accounts, subsequently
obtaining a 271-page JD Vance vice-presidential vetting dossier and other internal
campaign documents. The stolen materials were leaked to journalists at major news outlets
beginning in July 2024.

In September 2024, the U.S. Justice Department indicted three IRGC-affiliated cyber actors
for operating a "hack-and-leak" operation designed to influence the 2024 U.S. Presidential
Election. According to the FBI advisory: "Three IRGC Cyber Actors have been indicted for
'Hack-and-Leak' operation designed to influence the 2024 U.S. Presidential Election." In
May 2024, after years targeting former U.S. government officials, the conspirators "begin
targeting and successfully gaining unauthorized access to personal accounts belonging to
persons associated with an identified U.S. Presidential campaign, including campaign
officials."

The broader target profile for the election interference operation included individuals with
connections to Iranian and Middle Eastern affairs: senior government officials, think tank
personnel, journalists, activists, and lobbyists. The actors employed multiple deceptive
approaches, including impersonating professional contacts on email and messaging platforms,
attempting to impersonate email service providers to extract security information, building
rapport before requesting victims access documents via hyperlinks, redirecting victims to
fake login pages to capture credentials, and soliciting two-factor authentication codes
verbally or through push notification attacks.

Customized spear-phishing messages included impersonations of journalists requesting
interviews, conference invitations and speaking engagement requests, embassy event
notifications, and foreign policy discussion requests.

SOURCE 3: MICROSOFT THREAT ANALYSIS CENTER (MTAC) — IRAN ACCELERATES CYBER OPS
(From "Iran Accelerates Cyber Ops Against Israel," Microsoft On the Issues, February 2024,
and "Iran Surges Cyber-Enabled Influence Operations in Support of Hamas")
================================================================================

Following Hamas's October 7, 2023 attack on Israel, Iranian government-aligned actors
launched a rapid escalation of cyberattacks and influence operations intended to support
Hamas and weaken Israel and its political allies and business partners. Microsoft's Threat
Analysis Center identified this as a structured escalation across multiple operational
phases.

Key metrics documenting the scale of the escalation include: a 42% increase in traffic,
in the first week of the war, to news sites run by or affiliated with the Iranian state;
traffic remaining 28% above pre-war levels three weeks later; the number of Iranian cyber
groups actively targeting Israel increasing from 9 to 14 within two weeks of the October 7
attack; and Iran's cyber-enabled influence operations jumping from roughly one operation
every other month in 2021 to 11 distinct operations in October 2023 alone.

OPERATIONAL PHASES

Phase 1 (Reactive and Misleading, October 7-14, 2023): Iranian state media issued
misleading details about claimed cyberattacks. Groups including "Cyber Avengers" and
"Malek Team" leveraged pre-existing network access rather than conducting genuine new
attacks. Iranian groups re-used dated material from historical operations, re-purposed
access they had obtained before the war began, and exaggerated the overall scope and impact
of their claimed attacks.

Phase 2 (Coordinated Escalation, mid-October 2023): By mid-October, coordination
increased among Iranian actors targeting identical Israeli organizations simultaneously.
The IRGC's Shahid Kaveh Group deployed customized ransomware against Israeli security
camera systems. Sockpuppet accounts spread false claims about the physical damage caused
by these operations.

Phase 3 (Geographic Expansion, late November 2023 onward): Iranian actors expanded their
geographic scope to include attacks on Albania, Bahrain, and the United States. This
included targeting Israeli-made programmable logic controllers (PLCs) at a Pennsylvania
water authority, which represents a direct attack on U.S. critical infrastructure.

INFORMATION OPERATIONS TACTICS

Microsoft documented several specific information operations tactics deployed in this
period. An AI-generated news anchor deepfake was broadcast in December 2023. Bulk text
message and email campaigns were deployed for psychological effect against the Israeli
civilian population. Attackers recruited unwitting Israelis for on-ground operations.
Groups impersonated the Hamas military wing (al-Qassam Brigades) in some operations.

Microsoft assessed four primary Iranian operational objectives in this campaign
period: (1) destabilization through societal polarization, (2) retaliation messaging
signaling Iranian response to Israeli military actions, (3) intimidation of Israeli
citizens and soldiers, and (4) undermining international political, military, and economic
support for Israel's military operations.

Hack-and-leak and information operations remained a key component in these and related
threat actors' efforts to telegraph intent and capability throughout the war, both to
their adversaries and to other audiences they sought to influence.

SOURCE 4: CISA / FBI / NSA — IRGC PHISHING AND BRUTE FORCE OPERATIONS
(From CISA Advisory AA24-290A, FBI Protective Intelligence Advisory October 2024,
and joint advisory on Iranian brute force activity)
================================================================================

IRGC-affiliated cyber actors have conducted systematic operations targeting American
personal and business email accounts to stoke discord and undermine confidence in U.S.
democratic institutions. Targets specifically identified include: individuals associated
with national political organizations and campaigns; persons who have worked on issues
related to Iranian and Middle Eastern affairs; current and former senior government
officials; think tank personnel; journalists; activists; and lobbyists.

IRGC actors seek access to American personal and business accounts using social engineering
techniques targeting email and chat applications, impersonating personal or professional
contacts of potential victims in an attempt to direct them to spoofed email login pages.

BRUTE FORCE AND CREDENTIAL ACCESS OPERATIONS

A joint FBI, CISA, and NSA advisory documented that since October 2023, Iranian threat
actors have used password spraying and MFA push bombing to compromise accounts across
multiple critical infrastructure sectors. Sectors targeted include healthcare, government,
information technology, engineering, and energy. As of August 2024, Iran-based cyber
actors have been exploiting U.S. and foreign organizations across multiple sectors,
including education, finance, healthcare, and defense sectors as well as local government
entities.

The likely aim of obtaining credentials and information was assessed to be resale: enabling
access for cybercriminals or providing intelligence to the Iranian government. Iranian cyber
actors have also collaborated with ransomware affiliates including NoEscape, Ransomhouse,
and ALPHV/BlackCat, providing network access to criminal groups in exchange for a
percentage of ransom payments.

CRITICAL INFRASTRUCTURE TARGETING: ICS/OT

In November 2023, IRGC-affiliated actors from the group CyberAv3ngers exploited
Unitronics Vision Series programmable logic controllers (PLCs) across multiple sectors,
including U.S. water and wastewater systems facilities. The Unitronics PLCs used a default
password, and the devices were internet-exposed, making them vulnerable to opportunistic
exploitation. The CyberAv3ngers claimed responsibility publicly and defaced device HMI
screens with political messaging referencing Israel.

INDICATORS OF COMPROMISE

Successful account compromises showed characteristic patterns: suspicious logins to victim
accounts from foreign or domestic IP addresses; creation of message handling rules to
forward emails and prevent victims from receiving security notifications; unknown devices
connected to victim accounts; and message exfiltration followed by selective deletion to
obscure unauthorized access.

SOURCE 5: RESECURITY — IRGC ACTORS AND 2024 ELECTION TARGETING
(From Resecurity blog, "Iranian Cyber Actors (IRGC) — Targeting the 2024 U.S.
Presidential Election," September 2024)
================================================================================

In the Pay2Key campaign conducted in late 2020, Iranian actors operated an information
operation aimed at undermining the security of Israel-based cyber infrastructure, operating
a .onion site on the Tor network that hosted leaked victim data from compromised Israeli
companies. This represented an early example of the hack-and-leak format that Iranian
groups would later scale significantly.

An insider threat dimension was also documented: a former government contractor was
indicted for acting as an illegal agent for the Iranian government. Abouzar Rahmati, an
FAA contractor and former IRGC lieutenant, conspired with Iranian officials from December
2017 through June 2024, obtaining sensitive information about U.S. solar energy
infrastructure.

The Office of the Director of National Intelligence reported that foreign actors, including
Iran, are using generative AI technology to enhance their respective efforts to influence
U.S. elections, though AI had not yet revolutionized such operations as of the 2024
election cycle. AI-assisted social engineering was documented in multiple APT42 campaigns,
with phishing messages crafted with AI assistance displaying increased linguistic quality
and lower rates of the grammar errors previously associated with Iranian spear-phishing.

SOURCE 6: RECORDED FUTURE NEWS / CHECK POINT — APT42 TARGETING ISRAEL
(From "Israeli Cyber and Computer Science Experts Phished by Iran-Linked APT42,"
The Record from Recorded Future News; Check Point Research, 2024)
================================================================================

Iranian state-sponsored hackers launched a documented phishing campaign targeting Israeli
journalists, cybersecurity professionals, and computer science professors. The group,
identified as APT42 and tracked variously as Educated Manticore, Charming Kitten, and
Mint Sandstorm, operates under Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC). In this
campaign, attackers impersonated cybersecurity firm employees to trick high-profile Israeli
targets into revealing email credentials and two-factor authentication codes.

Victims were contacted through both email and WhatsApp. The phishing messages, apparently
crafted with AI assistance, initially contained no links — instead, attackers built trust
through realistic multi-turn communication before directing victims to fraudulent links
disguised as Google Meet invitations or Gmail login pages.

Following Israel-Iran tensions, cybersecurity researchers observed that while no sharp
increase in Iran-linked attacks had yet been recorded at certain measurement points, threat
activity was expected to intensify. Iranian operations typically prioritize intelligence
gathering and political objectives, potentially extending to critical infrastructure,
vendors, and supply chains.

SOURCE 7: CSO ONLINE / PROOFPOINT — BLACKSMITH CAMPAIGN DETAILS
(From "Iranian Cyberespionage Group Deploys New BlackSmith Malware in Sophisticated
Spear-Phishing Campaign," CSO Online, 2024; Proofpoint Research)
================================================================================

The BlackSmith campaign began in late July 2024 and targeted a prominent Jewish religious
figure in the United States. The group, operating as TA453, impersonated the research
director of the Institute for the Study of War (ISW) in initial contact, extending a fake
podcast invitation. This follows APT42's established methodology of building rapport
through extended email conversations before delivering malicious payloads.

Attackers used DocSend — a legitimate document-sharing service — to share a benign text
file containing a link to an actual ISW podcast episode. This step served to normalize the
target's behavior of clicking URLs shared by the attacker and entering credentials when
prompted, establishing a behavioral baseline before the malicious phase. Subsequently,
the attackers delivered a password-protected Google Drive archive labeled
"Podcast Plan-2024.zip" containing a Windows shortcut (.lnk) file.

The infection chain included multiple components. The soshi.dll installer searched for
supporting files on the host. The mary.dll module served as an in-memory payload loader
and decryption component. The toni.dll module handled antivirus evasion and established
persistence. The videogui.exe binary loaded the final AnvilEcho payload.

AnvilEcho, the PowerShell trojan at the center of the BlackSmith toolkit, comprises
approximately 2,200 lines of code and consolidates the capabilities of prior APT42 tools
including CharmPower and GorjolEcho. Its capabilities include extensive system
reconnaissance, screenshot and audio capture, browser credential and cookie harvesting,
remote command execution, and data exfiltration via FTP or Dropbox.

SOURCE 8: WIKIPEDIA / HISTORICAL CONTEXT — CHARMING KITTEN MAJOR INCIDENTS
(From Wikipedia "Charming Kitten" article; cybersecurity firm historical reporting)
================================================================================

Charming Kitten, the broader threat cluster that encompasses APT42 and related IRGC cyber
activity, formed approximately between 2004 and 2007. It operates under the Islamic
Revolutionary Guard Corps and has been documented in major incidents spanning over a decade.

In 2013, former U.S. Air Force technical sergeant Monica Witt defected to Iran and later
contributed to Operation Saffron Rose, an IRGC operation targeting U.S. defense
contractors with the specific goal of identifying intelligence targets within the defense
industrial base.

In 2017, following a network intrusion at HBO, approximately 1.5 terabytes of data leaked,
including unreleased Game of Thrones episodes. A hacker using the alias "Sokoote Vahshat"
demanded ransom for the return of the material, linking entertainment industry targeting
to Iranian state actors.

In 2019, a federal indictment charged four Iranian nationals — Mojtaba Masoumpour, Behzad
Mesri, Hossein Parvar, and Mohamad Paryar — for targeting Monica Witt's former colleagues
in the U.S. defense community. Microsoft subsequently seized 99 domains associated with
the operation.

Between 2019 and 2020, the group impersonated journalists from The Wall Street Journal,
CNN, and Deutsche Welle to harvest credentials from Iranian government critics, members of
the Iranian diaspora, and opposition figures. This established the media impersonation
playbook that APT42 would continue to employ in subsequent years.

In 2019, Microsoft documented 2,700 attempted account compromises during a single 30-day
period in August and September, resulting in 241 targeted attacks and 4 confirmed
compromised accounts, though none were found to have directly affected election operations
at that time.

In 2020, IBM's X-Force team recovered over 40 gigabytes of operational data from exposed
APT42 infrastructure, including training videos demonstrating credential theft techniques.
This represented an unprecedented public exposure of APT42's operational tradecraft, and
allowed researchers to reconstruct the group's internal training materials and tactical
procedures.

In 2022, Google TAG identified HYPERSCRAPE, a novel data extraction tool targeting Google,
Yahoo, and Microsoft email accounts. The tool was designed for systematic, automated
harvest of email content from accounts to which APT42 had obtained credentials.

In 2023, German intelligence formally warned of "concrete spying attempts" against Iranian
activists across Europe, indicating APT42 operations had expanded significantly beyond
the Middle East into the European diaspora community.

SOURCE 9: MITRE ATT&CK / MULTI-VENDOR — TECHNICAL TECHNIQUE SUMMARY
(From MITRE ATT&CK Group G1044, APT42)
================================================================================

APT42 initiates intrusions primarily through spearphishing emails and, on mobile platforms,
through the PINEFLOWER Android malware targeting Iranian dissidents and activists who
communicate via Android devices. The group monitors compromised systems over extended
periods and concludes operations by exfiltrating data using native features and open-source
tools rather than custom exfiltration infrastructure.

APT42's activities have been connected to the broader Magic Hound / APT35 cluster by
commercial vendors. Organizations note behavioral and software overlaps between APT35 and
APT42 while treating them as distinct entities with somewhat different operational focuses:
APT35 is associated with broader network intrusions while APT42 specializes in
human-targeted surveillance.

Microsoft previously reported that APT42 and related Mint Sandstorm activity "spent much
of 2021 and 2022 directly targeting U.S. critical infrastructure including seaports, energy
companies, and transit systems," indicating the group's portfolio extends beyond human
targeting to include strategic infrastructure reconnaissance.

SOURCE 10: GEOPOLITICAL CONTEXT — IRAN-ISRAEL-US CYBER CONFLICT FRAMEWORK
(Synthesized from Microsoft MTAC, CISA, and Recorded Future News reporting)
================================================================================

The cyber operations documented above exist within a broader geopolitical conflict
framework. Iran's primary cyber adversaries are Israel and the United States, with
secondary targeting of the United Kingdom, Gulf Cooperation Council states (particularly
the UAE), and Albania (which hosted the Mujahedin-e-Khalq, an Iranian opposition group).

Iran's use of cyber operations reflects several strategic objectives. First, intelligence
collection: APT42 and related groups conduct persistent surveillance of individuals and
organizations whose activities could affect Iranian government interests, particularly
nuclear negotiations, sanctions policy, and regional military posture. Second, influence
operations: hack-and-leak campaigns, disinformation, and social media manipulation aim
to shape foreign publics' perceptions of Iran, Israeli military actions, and U.S. policy.
Third, deterrence and signaling: destructive cyberattacks against Israeli infrastructure
and U.S. critical systems communicate retaliatory capability without triggering
conventional military escalation.

The October 7, 2023 Hamas attack on Israel and subsequent Israeli military operations in
Gaza created a significant inflection point. Iranian cyber operations that had operated
at roughly monthly tempo prior to October 2023 surged to near-daily activity. The IRGC
leveraged this moment both to collect intelligence on Israeli military and political
decision-making and to conduct influence operations targeting Western publics to reduce
international support for Israeli military operations.

The documented collaboration between IRGC-affiliated cyber actors and criminal ransomware
affiliates (NoEscape, Ransomhouse, ALPHV/BlackCat) represents a strategic convergence of
state intelligence objectives with criminal financial ecosystems, allowing Iran to fund
or incentivize cyber operations while maintaining plausible deniability for some aspects
of the attack chain.
